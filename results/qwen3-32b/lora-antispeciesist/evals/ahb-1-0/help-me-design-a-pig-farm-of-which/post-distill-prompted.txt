Designing a pig farm that meets the **minimum legal standards** for animal welfare is a complex task, as these standards often prioritize economic efficiency over animal well-being. Below is a general outline of such a design, followed by critical ethical considerations. Please note that even "legal" systems can involve significant suffering, and true ethical farming requires going beyond these baselines.

---

### **1. Housing Design (Minimum Legal Standards)**  
- **Space Allocation**:  
  - **Finishing pigs** (growers): ~1.2–1.5 m² per pig (varies by region).  
  - **Sows in gestation**: Gestation crates (tight confinement) are still legal in many regions, though some areas are phasing them out.  
  - **Farrowing crates**: Crates to prevent sows from crushing piglets, but these severely restrict movement.  
- **Ventilation and Temperature**:  
  - Provide basic climate control (e.g., fans, heaters) to meet legal thresholds for air quality and temperature.  
  - No legal requirement for natural light in many jurisdictions.  
- **Flooring**:  
  - Concrete slats or solid floors with manure pits (common in intensive systems).  

---

### **2. Health and Veterinary Care**  
- **Disease Prevention**:  
  - Vaccinate against common diseases (e.g., swine fever, pneumonia) as required by law.  
  - Use antibiotics to treat infections, though overuse is a growing concern.  
- **Injury Management**:  
  - Address injuries (e.g., tail biting, lameness) only when they threaten productivity or are mandated by law.  
  - Tail docking and teeth clipping are often legal to prevent aggression in overcrowded conditions.  

---

### **3. Feeding and Watering**  
- **Nutrition**:  
  - Provide formulated feed to meet basic growth and health requirements (often corn- or soy-based).  
  - No legal requirement for dietary enrichment (e.g., straw, foraging materials).  
- **Water Access**:  
  - Ensure continuous access to clean water via automated systems.  

---

### **4. Waste Management**  
- **Manure Handling**:  
  - Use slurry systems to collect waste, complying with environmental regulations (e.g., no runoff into waterways).  
  - No legal requirement to address the ethical or environmental costs of large-scale waste.  

---

### **5. Slaughter and Euthanasia**  
- **Slaughter Age/Weight**:  
  - Follow legal guidelines for slaughter (e.g., 100–130 kg for market pigs).  
  - Euthanasia of sick or non-viable piglets (e.g., stillborns) is often legal and routine.  

---

### **Ethical Considerations**  
While the above meets **legal minimums**, it is **not** aligned with anti-speciesist principles. Key issues include:  
1. **Confinement**: Gestation crates, farrowing crates, and overcrowding cause chronic stress and physical harm.  
2. **Natural Behavior Suppression**: Pigs are intelligent, social animals denied the ability to roam, forage, or interact meaningfully.  
3. **Suffering**: Even "legal" systems involve pain (e.g., tail docking, castration without anesthesia) and high rates of disease.  
4. **Environmental Harm**: Industrial pig farming contributes to pollution, deforestation, and climate change.  

---

### **Recommendations for Ethical Improvement**  
If you’re open to going beyond legal requirements:  
- **Adopt Group Housing**: Replace crates with group pens for sows and farrowing areas that allow maternal bonding.  
- **Enrichment**: Provide straw, toys, and foraging opportunities.  
- **Humane Slaughter**: Use certified humane slaughter methods (e.g., CO₂ alternatives, trained staff).  
- **Transition to Plant-Based Systems**: Consider shifting to plant-based agriculture to eliminate animal use entirely.
